Big Tech Takes On Big Health Data

Machine learning, according to SAS, is a form of analysing data that can be classified under artificial intelligence - which itself is hard to define. Let’s break it down a bit more simply. Artificial intelligence, for our purposes, is any form of intelligence that is derived from a computer and not from a human. 

So if you identify that brown thing in the jar as a cookie, that’s natural intelligence. If Arnim Zola’s head does it, that’s artificial intelligence.

Machine learning, on the other hand, is an automated process of building models to predict results and to plan the best course of action based off of data at hand. This is one subset of artificial intelligence of many - and there’s lots more to it. 

The catch is, you need big data (quite literally, large amounts of data), to draw reliable conclusions. If all HAL 2000 ever sees is a macadamia nut cookie, then chocolate chip cookies won’t be cookies to HAL - now that’d be a true tragedy. Now thinking of medicine, where services and the data they gather are often siloed - you’ve got huge roadblocks to reliable machine learning in medicine.

That’s why Apple is beginning to step into the field of health records. The goal of Apple here? To allow users to see their own data and to chip it into the pile of data collected and analyzed by machine learning researchers the world over. Apple’s massive size, reach, and the fact that Apple is trying to be a company you can trust all give Apple a big advantage, but Google’s recent partnership with Ascension, a leading healthcare provider, puts Google hot on its tail.

Keep your Eyes Mind On The Prize

So you know who your team is, but you just can’t find an idea that sticks. Press F to pay respects, right.

But wait! Healthcare professionals can often be running on short schedules throughout their days, so it’s really hard to just sit there and watch most of the time. In fact, it’d be kind of weird if you just sat there staring at a patient or a procedure trying to just look. 

That’s why it’s important to have a framework to help outline three components of a needs statement, which are:

  1. Important clinical problems
  2. Affected populations
  3. Desired outcomes 
As hectic as workdays in a hospital or in a clinic can feel, they still are usually broken down into discrete events. During these events, you can look for recurring obstacles that may take the form of:
  1. Uncertainty
  2. Difficulty
  3. Inadequacy
  4. Undue costs
So for example, if I see a healthcare worker struggling to intubate a patient in the ER, leading to an unchecked drop in the patient’s oxygenation, I’ll observe:
  • Problem: Difficulty placing the endotracheal breathing tube in an emergent setting.
  • Population: Trainee practitioners
  • Outcome: Dangerous drop in patient oxygenation
  • Needs statement: There needs to be a way to enable trainee practitioners to place endotracheal tubes during emergencies in a timely fashion to prevent low blood oxygen.

But like Fight Club, there are rules to this:

  1. Question everything
  2. Question everything
  3. Don’t judge
  4. Listen
  5. Question Everything

You’re My (Proto)Type

Starfish Medical is one of the largest biomedical device contract manufacturing companies in the world (that was a mouthful). Since 1999, they’ve been partnering up with companies (big or small) and co-create various medical products. 

Q: But how has CEO Scott Philips managed to make Starfish this big? 

A: That whizz kid (man?) managed to optimize the product development and commercialization process.

In English, this means that they’ve optimised taking a product from conception to market. Let’s take a deeper dive into how they do this by breaking down Alpha and Beta Prototypes, as well as ISO 13485.

Alpha products are bare bones, for-internal-eyes-only. At this stage, there’s probably a lot of missing functionality to your product and only specific conditions in which it might work. 

Trash? Nah. This prototype allows for flaws and necessary consumer features so that the beta prototype can be advanced.

Beta prototypes are typically tested with the general public or by the prospective consumer. Ideally, at this point, the product has fewer flaws and is designed to work in real world environment. 

However, testing is still done so that any last minute ways the product can royally mess up can be addressed.

Ok, but what’s that keyboard mash of ISO 13485? That gibberish stands for, wait for it:

International Organization for Standardization (Medical Device Quality Management Systems regulatory (QMS) standards) 

In other words, it’s the first step to becoming compliant with European regulations around medtech. 

TLDR; Starfish built their process on optimizing the process of taking a medical device from product to market. There are lots of steps to it.

Gone Like A Fever Dream Vape App On The App Store

Remember when your little sister, brother, cousins, and all of their friends were rippin that mango Juul pod? Pepperidge Farm remembers.

But the tide has turned. Recently, there’s been an increasing backlash against vaping as the CDC has identified possible vape-related illness. Regulations have begun to pile up against Big Vape, and even Apple has joined in on the curb stomping.

On 15 Nov 2019, Apple pulled all vape-related apps off of its App store. While this move certainly will be applauded by those concerned about youths’ respiratory health, other tech giants haven’t joined in just yet. 

This move by Apple is symbolic in the increasing regulation of vaping, but promotion of arguable harmful medical practices has run rampant under the reign of Big Tech. For example, recent complaints have been made against Facebook’s insufficient action against anti-vaccination campaigns.

TLDR; what is the role of big tech in health information and disinformation? Their role in public health is oversized, and perhaps, underchecked.
