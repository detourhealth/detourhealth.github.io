Digitizing Drug Development - the Next Frontier?

Drugs are so very important to the ability of modern medicine to manage and treat a vast assortment of medical maladies, but the process of thinking up, designing, and producing a drug can cost billions of dollars. 

This high cost has been used as a justification for the high medication prices that patients, insurance companies, and public medical systems face. 

What’s interesting though, is that recently, Novartis, makers of of clozapine, diclofenac, and other fame, partnered with  Microsoft to develop digital frameworks for drug discovery and development.

So what does this mean? It could mean that drugs that are more specific, and as such, have fewer side effects could be developed.

Ideally, it would also mean that the patients we treat could get more effective drugs more quickly and more cheaply. Learn more here.

Will this manifest as real-life benefits to the drug development process? Novartis certainly thinks so - it’s been buying up health sciences data analytics companies such as Shyft Analytics left and right in the past few years. 

INNOVATION PEARL

Know thy Enemy, but also thy Market and Competition

Okay, so you’ve got your team, you know what your skills are, and you’ve got matching skintight leotards - what’s next?

According to the Stanford Biodesign process, it’s important to know what exactly you’re dealing with so that you can treat the proper pathology. You wouldn’t go ahead with that ORIF (don’t forget the ancef!) without ensuring that the bone really IS broken. Right? Right??

Needs finding: Acceptance criteria

Beyond what your team can do, it’s important to acknowledge what makes a project attractive to you and your team - hence the need for acceptance criteria.

What defines an acceptance criterion is really up to you - but take for example, market conditions. If you go into a high competition area, like EMRs, you’re bound to find need, but you’ll also face competition (of varying quality). But if you go into a low competition area, like robot assisted laparascopic surgery (shoutout to Intuitive Surgical), then you might find less need and higher barriers and lower competition. 

TLDR; it’s important to assess external factors and other criteria beyond what your team thinks it can do to find the best niche for innovation for you.

That's a Sick Necklace Dude? Where'd You Get it?

This time, we’re checking out CloudDx, a Canadian company co-developed by an ICU doc, Sunny Kohli.

The team behind CloudDx works to leverage its strong business background to identify key needs in the telemedicine market. Specifically, they’re gunning for the Qualcomm Tricorder Xprize for $10 mil with this device.

While it’s almost as cool looking as Ye’s chains, it’s debatably more functional because it acts as a:

  • ECG
  • Photoplethysmograph
  • Core Temp Sensor
  • Accelerometer
  • Gyroscopic Sensor

If that wasn’t enough it can differentiate between 7 different respiratory diseases by the sound of a patient’s cough. At this rate, this necklace is probably a better studied MSI than me…
