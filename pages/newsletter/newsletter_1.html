Digital Health - Will it Really Work?

Digital health is all the hype; it uses all types of technology to provide healthcare data to patients and physicians. It’s working to improve diagnostic ability, physician productivity, and save money for the system. 

Hold on a minute… this doesn’t seem like our reality. There remains:

  1. A lack of clear policies for how doctors should use digital health solutions

  2. A lack of clear and consistent reimbursement guidelines for telehealth

  1. A lack of adequate tech training among physician teams, leading to lost opportunities to recoup hours currently devoted to administrative tasks

In fact, not everyone is convinced. A survey of 284 healthcare and health tech-involved providers was conducted in July and August this year. 

  • 45% said they weren’t considering a partnership with digital health due to undemonstrated value
  • 79% said they were worried or somewhat concerned about data breaches
  • 47% of surveyed individuals said that startups in the digital health sector don’t have a complete understanding of the sector they are trying to enter

It’s time we start addressing some of these concerns. Read more here.

By involving healthcare professionals early in the development process, innovative solutions can be better-designed. Let’s work to create a community that encourages change.

TLDR; digital health = good, implementation = not going so well

A Journey Begins With A Strategic Focus

Where the heck do you begin when planning to improve something in healthcare? Is there an algorithm to launch a medical startup? Can someone page Internal and ask them? 

The Stanford Biodesign process is a slick way the cool kids down South think about medical innovation so that they can avoid common pitfalls and increase success. Unfortunately, or fortunately, we’re not all Archimedes in that we don’t discover things by a pure stroke of genius. Or in the bathtub. That’s beside the point. 

We’ve summarized neatly part of what the nerds at Stanford said below. Basically, it’s important to identify a strategic focus - an area to pursue that matches your core competencies and personal or organizational mission.

This strategic focus begins with a personal inventory that includes:

  1. A Mission statement: A broad directional aspiration which defines what you want to achieve.

  2. Strengths and weaknesses: Who’s really good at coding? Who memorised the entire UptoDate page on stroke treatment algorithms? You need to know who’s good at what.

Apparently, this personal inventory allows the team to be mindful of what they want to do, and what they can do. Only then will it be clear as to what goals are compatible with your interests, and whether or not your team can do it. Sounds Zen, eh?

Let’s Get Together And Feeeel Alright

I love me some Bob Marley as much as the next guy, but one of Canada’s own superstar physician entrepreneurs really puts a new spin on getting together and feeling alright.

Alexandra Greenhill, a physician-CEO started Careteam Technologies, a Vancouver-based company that allows all physicians working with a patient to connect. 

In addition to allowing everyone to post-to-dos and supporting transitions in care, slick AI features have been added so that no follow ups are missed and so that patients and their families can remain engaged in care.

While this company itself solves a problem that all physicians will face at some point in their careers, Dr. Greenhill herself is even more badass. She has won many awards such as the Cartier Women’s Initiative Awards Laureate, the WXN’s Top 100 Most Powerful Women in Canada, and the Queen Elizabeth II Diamond Jubilee Medal.
