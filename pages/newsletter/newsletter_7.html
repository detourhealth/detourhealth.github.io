INNOVATION PEARL

How to condense everything you need into 1 sentence
“All good things [needs finding processes] must come to an end.” So, you’ve done your homework and you have a solid understanding of the problem or need you’d like to address. What’s next?

The next step is to create a needs statement! How? Creating a needs statement is an iterative process that bridges your identified problem, the specific population being addressed, and a clear, measurable outcome (THROWBACK TO SMART GOALS) into an accurate, descriptive, and solution-independent clinical statement.


As an example, if you identify urinary tract infections secondary to prolonged catheterization in the ICU as a problem you’d like to address, you might come up with a statement like this: “A way to reduce the incidence of urinary tract infections in ICU patients that reduces hospital stay duration.” Notice how this statement doesn’t limit how you fix the problem.

“Why tho?” - Detour readers, maybe

A needs statement translates your need into a goal-directed summary which your team can use to compare and decide which need represents the most compelling opportunities, and, thus, is worth pursuing.

So how do you go about making a good needs statement anyway? Here are some helpful suggested steps from the folks over at Stanford Biodesign:

  1. Write a preliminary needs statement: Much like a medical student, this draft does not have to be perfect, as long as it contains the three essential components, it will act as a prototype to build your final product on. One option to get your team going is to treat it like a game, such as Mad Lib, to fill in the blanks of a template; e.g. “A way to address [problem] in [population] that [outcome].” Then select the best draft and go forward from there!

  2. Needs Scoping: This is a process by which you and your team can dive deeper to try out different levels of focus or specificity. Starting with your draft, you can ask the following questions:

  • Is the problem just what we outlined or can it be broader?

  • Is the issue relevant to a larger population? Or is our issue more relevant when applied to a smaller/more specific population?

  • Is the outcome stated the most essential or is another outcome more compelling?

  • Is the need, as scoped, consistent with the team’s strategic focus?

  1. Avoid embedding solutions: A needs statement is a tool to identify a problem and the meaningful outcome you’d like your solution to result in. This gives you and your team something to build a solution around. Embedding a solution in a needs statement only works to limit you. An example of an embedded solution would be: “A way to ultrasonically weld sutures in surgery” (limited to one approach). A better statement would be: “A way to secure an aortic valve prosthesis with minimal or no on-pump time to reduce cognitive dysfunction after surgery” (focused on outcome measure rather than approach).

 Other pitfalls to watch out for include:

  • Need that is too general or too specific

  • Need that is too rooted in current practice

  • Need that focuses on a negative (e.g. “A way to not have infections related to dialysis catheters”)

TLDR; A needs statements helps guide your team and the solution finding process. To write one, identify the problem, population, and outcome, then decide as a team the appropriate level of scope for each and avoid embedding a solution in your statement!

Is your crush sending your heart into an arrhythmia? Not to worry, the folks over at Karidum have a solution for you!

Kardium is a local Vancouver biotechnology company currently working on a spicy new device that offers a “complete solution for the treatment of Atrial Fibrillation” in a single device.

Sounds like a big deal? That’s because it is.

Atrial fibrillation is one of the most common heart disorders with at least 2.7 million Americans currently living with it.  Left untreated, the condition leads to an increase in the production of blood clots, which can break off and cause strokes and/or heart failure. 

Kardium is serving up their solution to the problem with all the trimmings. Their device, called “The Globe System” offers rapid pulmonary vein isolation, high-resolution mapping and atrial ablation.  Current alternatives don’t offer the high-resolution mapping that the system can provide and also do not offer all three systems in one device.

So excited for this device to come out that it is giving you palpitations? Not to worry, it’s almost available!

The company is currently seeking approval for a CE mark, which would allow the device to be sold in Europe.  You can read about their latest clinical results here.
BREAK ROOM BANTS

Ever wondered what your brain is actually thinking?

Shower Thought: If we could talk to our brains would we be talking to ourselves or just a part of ourselves?

Much to my chagrin, nobody is trying to invent a way to talk to our brains or answer my deep, philosophical shower thoughts. However, Interaxon is leveraging EEG technology to do actual useful things. Founded in Toronto in 2012, Interaxon beautifully demonstrates the importance of applying existing technology to new markets by taking EEG technology and applied it to everyday life. They added on brain computer interface features to allow people to wear their device and control things such as the lights and musical ensembles. They initially crowdfunded $300k on Indiegogo to get going. Interaxon has taken their devices, Muse, and pivoted to focus on the wellness community. It’s now used to augment and guide meditation. You wear the EEG-type headband, and it analyzes your brain waves during meditation to redirect you to focus. 

While Interaxon was not founded by physician-entrepreneurs, it has several MD and psychology expert advisors. It’s now being studied by the research community to see how meditation changes the brain, affects attention, and improves systemic symptoms such as headaches and pain management.
