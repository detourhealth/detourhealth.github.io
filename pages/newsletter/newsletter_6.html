Virtual Healthcare for Patients

There’s currently lots of buzz around online doctor consultations. However, much like the Canucks in the past few seasons, it doesn't come without its issues, challenges and bad players. In fact, other countries including the US and UK are marching down the era of integrating “telehealth” into their healthcare systems.

Which includes includes telephone visits, secure messaging (e mail, text), and video conferencing. The hype generated from the US and UK is stirring Revolutionary war action in Canada (mild but important). This phenomena can be observed with the 2018 study by Canada Health Infoway showing that 71% of Canadians would like to book appointments electronically but only 9% of practitioners offered this. 

The benefits of telehealth are abundant and obvious, patients get to avoid sometime grueling visits to their GP for simple tasks like diagnosing a cold. Additionally, it would offer an alternative route for the elderly, those in rural areas or even those who cannot take sick days off work , to seek out help from specialists. 

Of course, we hear you! this comes with the loss of the person-person contact as well as added complexity if the videoconference determines a further in person visit is required, they’ll be conducting 2 visits rather than 1 decreasing the efficiency of the overall system. 

Currently, there are 3 main challenges outlined by the Canadian Medical Association that need to be tackled for widespread uptake of virtual care. 

  1. Navigating practice restrictions on providing care to patients in different provinces/ territories. Physicians are allowed to provide emergency care in other jurisdictions, but day-to-day care typically requires prior approval to be insured.

  2. Implementing appropriate compensation mechanisms for physicians in a public healthcare system with provincial healthcare plans. Doctors of BC has outlined the fee plans for local patients from approved telemedicine facilities, but this still needs to be sorted for specialists and out of province visits.

  3. Solving the siloed healthcare system and interoperability present between patients, physicians, and health facilities. Currently, services allow patients to be seen by any GP, not your own GP, and ensuring the reporting and follow up care can be provided is challenging without one connected system.

Check out the CMA report for more info!

INNOVATION PEARL

Needs finding: Conducting interviews

“Innovators tend to go out and ask doctors what they want rather than observe what they need. When you talk to physicians, as well as others involved in the delivery of care, you’ve got to learn the difference between what they say, what they want, what they’ll pay for, and what they actually do.“  Thomas Fogarty

This means that when conducting interviews for the next big thing, you need to know that it is imperative to ask the right questions. This will allow you to  further flesh out where a new solution could help. Try to get your observations to conspire with the interviewer's questions. To do so, we have provided the following interview guidelines: 

  • Ask why
  • Never say “usually” when asking a question, ask about specific instances
  • Encourage stories
  • Look for inconsistencies
  • Pay attention to nonverbal cues
  • Do not be afraid of silence
  • Do not suggest answers to questions
  • Avoid binary questions
  • Be prepared to document
When you’ve heard enough experiences, try to explore the next step which involves transitioning problems, opportunities, and outcomes into needs and conduct a value exploration to see where you can maximize economic value and therefore intervene with an innovative solution. The following value signposts can be used to identify value-related opportunities during clinical immersion: 
  • Practice based markers:
    • Potential to keep a patient out of the hospital and/or ER
    • Potential to shorten patient’s length of hospital stay
    • Potential to reduce procedure time or resource/staff for a given intervention
  • Budget-based markers:
    • Diagnostics treatments or procedures that are significantly less expensive in other geographies
    • Diagnostics treatments or procedures in which technology is a high percentage of total cost 
    • Conditions for which life-long cost of care is especially high
DEEP DIVE

I Hear Your Heart Beat to the Beat of the Drums

It turns out Ke$ha was right, an exciting new development in ultrasound technology uses capacitive micromachined ultrasonic transducers (CMUT) which act as tiny drums, replacing the traditional piezoelectric transducers as the source of ultrasonic waves for ultrasound machines.

You may be wondering, what’s the big deal here?

Recent studies using this technology show that it is possible to reduce the amount of computational power required to acquire an image, while increasing quality of said image.  A great example can be found in You et al. who discuss one method of implementing CMUTs to get better images.  As ultrasound gets better, it looks like less of us will be “dying young.”  Sorry Ke$ha.

TLDR; Tiny drums are being used in ultrasounds offering an improved image quality over current technology.
BREAK ROOM BANTS

AI Triage - What's Next?

Babylon Health, the (in)famous company that is aiming to spearhead digital health delivery the world around, is coming to the USA in January 2020. Specifically, it hopes to begin to help triage patients as it already does in countries like Canada and Rwanda. 

Ok, but why is triage important anyway? Can’t we all just go to the ER when we’re sick or injured? 

I mean, my stubbed toe really hurts and your chest pain has been happening for a few hours so it really can wait, right?

What’s really cool about the Babylon Health triage system is that it:

  1. Allows for less life-threatening illnesses and injuries to be managed by lower-acuity services like family doctors
  2. Ensures that minimal staffing resources are used in the triage process because the triage system offered by Babylon is not limited by the number of nurses and doctors at hand.

There’s a catch, though: Babylon is rolling out for Medicaid populations first in the multi-player healthcare system in the USA. Medicaid is available for limited income and resource populations, which begs the question:

Will Medicaid populations receive better or worse care than non-Medicaid populations because of Babylon? 

P.S. Check out the 🌶️ claim by Babylon that its triaging service performs better than family doctors
