Do The Hokey Pokey... And Start A VR Venipuncture Company

Google Glass. Apple Glasses. Oculus Rift. VR is gaining traction and has applications in gaming, in making it harder for those of us who are directionally challenged to get lost, and in teaching. But is there any direct role for VR/AR in healthcare?

Let’s break it down. What is VR - also known as virtual reality? For our purposes, it’s the creation of an artificial environment for people to interact with. The most glaring application for this is improving accuracy in surgery and in training healthcare workers how to effectively carry out procedures. 

Augmented Reality, also known as AR, puts information into your field of view, kind of like the HUD in Halo. Only instead of knowing what your shield integrity is at, you’ll know what your patient’s vital signs are, and instead of seeing the distance to a checkpoint, you see the texts from DoorDash saying that your courier has arrived but you’re still in the OR. 

One of the most awesome demonstrations of the power of AR is that harnessed by Accuvein, a company whose product maps the veins from under the skin onto the person’s skin so that a 63% reduction in pokes can occur. 

So thanks to Accuvein, instead of ye olde grandma getting her blood drawn after 5 needle pokes, blood can be drawn on the first time. 

What’s exciting about this is that it’s only the beginning of what we can do with VR/AR in health. Got any cool applications you can think of? Think Accuvein is bogus? Let us know!

INNOVATION PEARL

I Got New Notes I Count'Em

Laying eyes on what goes on in a healthcare setting is important, but so is being able to articulate what you saw effectively and objectively. 

It’s hard to ensure that what went on is objectively documented without editorialisation; our perception of reality is difficult, if not impossible to separate from the straight facts. That’s why a systematic way of documenting healthcare observations is so very important. Let’s break it down a little below.

E.g. The patient was laid flat on the table. The physician’s assistant sterilized the groin area. Then, the doctor tried to gain vascular access through the groin. This took multiple attempts. The doctor mentioned that this was because the vessels were deep and nonpalpable. The patient seemed to experience pain each time the needle was inserted, and the physician became increasingly frustrated.

Some important points to keep salient in any of your notes are:

  1. Patient and provider perspectives 
  2. Healthcare system perspectives
  3. Procedural details such as timing and equipment necessary
  4. Questions about people or their behaviour to be investigated
TLDR; healthcare observation notes are important, and should be very procedurally organized to gather as much unbiased, informative data as possible.

The Pen Is Mightier Than The ELISA

ELISA, or enyme-linked immuno-sorbent assays, are tests that allow for the detection of molecules in a very sensitive and specific manner. What’s the catch? They’re expensive, and they require skilled technicians in order to be run properly. In summary, not exactly grade 9 science experiment material. 

You know what does sound like Grade 9 science experiment material? Developing a sensitive test that is comparable to an ELISA that just uses a paper and pen. 

I’m not joking. 

Bahavarnia et al. developed a paper based assay to detect cancer-associated antigen-125 (CA-125). This antigen is associated with ovarian cancer and currently requires ELISA for detection. 

Now the cool stuff happens here. Bahavarnia and their group use a regular old pen to distribute conductive bio-ink onto photographic paper to create an electrode, which was stabilised with conductive nanoparticles. CA-125 antibodies, which specifically bind to CA-125 were then adhered to the electrode.

(For those who don’t speak engineering, an electrode is a conductor through which electricity enters and leaves) 

So all in all, as a result of CA-125 binding to CA-125 antibodies binding to the electrode, a change in the current from baseline can be noted. Through this change, we can therefore detect the concentration of CA-125 in a given solution. This has implications for making ovarian cancer detection easier, cheaper, and more sensitive. 

TLDR; Paper is cheaper than immunochemical assays, and this might just shake up the world of early ovarian cancer detection.
 
BREAK ROOM BANTS

Meds For Hims, Meds For Hers, Meds For Everyone!

Not everyone likes popping into the doc’s office, and now, you can be even more of a hermit if you don’t want to talk about issues that might be hard to talk about! 

Whether or not you’re concerned about hair loss, sexual dysfunction, or hot flashes, Ro, a company specialising in remote consultations for medication orders, has it all to temporary settle your anxieties. As long as you don’t think about how the mailman might know what he’s delivering.

Ro recently raised $85 million USD in order to expand its offering to smoking cessation and health worries related to female aging. Its in stiff competition with Hims, which is a company that also started in the space of mail-order erectile dysfunction drugs. However, Hims has recently diverged to serve the mail-order birth control and female libido enhancement drug (finbanserin) market.

This all goes to question; what is the role of doctors beyond prescription of medications for health woes? Are there times during which it’s better to be served by a mail order service than by an actual physician? What is the role of healthcare workers overall in our tech enabled, knowledge free-for-all society? Is this existential crisis time for healthcare workers? 

TLDR; male, female, and smoking cessation mail-order health product companies are booming. What does this mean for the role of doctors in monitoring and maintaining patient health?
