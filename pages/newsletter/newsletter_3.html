The Robots Are Coming! The Robots are Coming! - Paul Revere, Probably

You’ve heard all the hype AI, or artificial intelligence in medicine; that it’ll take away radiologists jobs, that it’s going to reduce the workload physicians have, and that it’ll improve patient care overall.

Well first off, what is AI? Tesler’s Theorem says that “it’s whatever hasn’t been done yet.” I think that it’s Skynet. So far, AI exists mainly in the arena of programs that can conduct functions that humans can’t do very well or efficiently - so for now, let’s define it as that.

A recent survey showed that AI is most likely to be used in medicine to automate business processes and to solve operational challenges that require lots of time and parsing lots of data - in the form of managing EHRs, for example. This might mean that your doc has more time to spend on complicated patients, and to eat that free call room ice cream -  a win-win for everyone. 

But let’s look at the next decade - during which our people will get older, our system more overburdened, and our healthcare costs will skyrocket. AI, in this context, may leverage information on age, gender, environment, comorbidities, genomics, and wearable data to transform a mass of information into preventative and predictive healthcare.

TLDR; HAL 9000 will give you more time now, but could help your patient know if he has pancreatic cancer earlier on in the coming decades.

INNOVATION PEARL


I Can Show You The World...Once I Really Know What’s In it.

If Aladdin could show us where needs in healthcare systems are, he’d be one helluva popular man. Unfortunately, he doesn’t exist, but the Stanford Biodesign Process does! (Geez, are these guys Stanford Biodesign shills or something?)

Before we dive in to solving a problem, we need to understand the various aspects of it. We can usually break problems down into these three categories:

  1. What is the healthcare issue at hand?
  2. What is the population affected by the problem?
  3. What is the desired outcome for the target population?
So how the heck do you answer these three questions in a manner that’s useful to your problem-solving mission? You can br-br-break down the methods you can use into:
  1. Background research on disease states, existing solutions, market factors, etc.
  2. Direct observations and interviews that you should do after you’ve done some research so that you can craft some questions
Still befuddled? Yeah, so are we. Well, to give you a head start some questions you can ask could be:
  1. What did the patient have to undergo in terms of preoperative tests?
  2. How much discomfort did the patient experience?
  3. What are the most complications of the procedure?
Obviously, there are many more questions you can ask that can be tailored to the problem at hand. It takes a little time and a little thinking, but this research process is invaluable in terms of deciding which problems to solve and how.

Under Pressure, I've Been Feeling Under Pressure - Logic.

Wound care often requires a multidisciplinary team that requires homecare nurses, physicians, and other professionals. 

Often, these players rotate in and out of care teams, which can leave new team members confused as to how the wound healing progressed.

Swift Medical Inc., founded in part by a McGill dermatology resident, uses AI to better-capture 3D characteristics of wounds in photos. In addition, the AI encourages an up to date recording of wound healing progress. 

Beyond wound tracking, this company’s product has applications in reducing pressure ulcer prevalence by involving multidisciplinary teams early to minimize these costly complications.
