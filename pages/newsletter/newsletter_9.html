INNOVATION PEARL

Needs Screening: Existing Solutions

Say you are several years into painstakingly putting together your new, super genius defibrillator. Then, the day before your team decides to take it to market, you see on the news that Dr. Someonelse with her CEO Shirly Notyou have just entered your local market with an even flashier, more efficient defibrillator at half the cost of your device.

Shocking and devastating. But could this have been avoided? Certainly! The second step of the Needs Screening process, which we started discussing in the last issue, is a thorough dive into market competitors to identify existing and upcoming solutions. Not only is this step vital for understanding the market and identifying where the most compelling opportunities are, but having taken this step BEFORE inventing your solution allows your team to specify your needs statement more effectively.

So how do you make sure you haven’t missed anything? It may help to systematically find existing solutions under each of these broad categories: diagnostics, behavioral/lifestyle treatment, pharmacologic or biologic solutions, percutaneous treatments, minimally invasive treatments, open surgery, psychosocial services, and disease progression monitoring. Once you have a good understanding of the existing solutions, it is important to then identify what gaps still exist - whether that be in terms of desired outcomes, servicing specific populations, or cost to the system or the patient.

 

TLDR; Before you can begin finding solutions and investing time in an idea, it is critical to research and understand existing solutions as well as what gaps still exist which your solution could target.
BREAK ROOM BANTS

Collaboration at it's finest


Here’s one you won’t hear very often from the brooding elderly, smartphones and portable devices are being used to improve the health of children.

Ottawa based pediatric otolaryngologist, Dr. Matthew Bromwich is combining his savvy academic mind with a drive for medical innovation at the Children’s Hospital of Eastern Ontario Research Institute. 

What else that brain do? Cost savings of course! Dr. Bromwich’s lab created a smartphone adaptor for any endoscope to eliminate the need for the traditional video tower. Physicians can record the scope on their phone and use it to diagnose patients, look back at later, and teach students.

But wait there’s more!

His lab then created Shoebox, an at home audiometry device so kids can test their own hearing. It uses a headset and ipad games to replace traditional testing methods. This device has huge potential in resource-limited settings where not everyone has access to professional hearing tests. It’s being used to do just that in Uganda, Bangladesh, Iqaluit and locally in Ottawa. 

So how does Shoebox have a leg up on the competition? It comes down to the fact that it is flexible and easy to use so OTs, family physicians, ENTs, audiologists, and patients and customers can reliably use the tool anywhere in the world. And TELUS seemed to agree, giving the product the award for mHelath Innovation in 2014.
